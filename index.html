Work in Progress

Misallocation in Banking

Solo-authored (draft coming soon)

We investigate how frictions impede efficient allocation of deposits across banks and reduce welfare generated by the banking sector. We propose a model featuring heterogeneous banks, borrowers, and depositors, and highlight the critical roles of market power, market segmentation, and regulatory constraints. Calibrating the model to U.S. banking data, we quantify (1) the welfare gain achievable through deposit reallocation by a social planner, and (2) the relative contribution of these specific frictions and their interactions to deposit misallocation in the banking sector.

Banks as Multi-Product Balance-Sheet Platforms

with Germán Gutiérrez and Hossein Poorvasei (draft coming soon)

This paper develops a theory of banks as multi-product balance-sheet platforms — firms that jointly produce a variety of assets and liabilities using a joint-production technology that links banks' marginal costs to both sides of the balance sheet. We estimate the model structurally for U.S. banks using Call Report data and show that technological complementarities between deposit-taking and loan-making explain most of the variation in spreads, monetary-policy pass-through, and bank value. A counterfactual that severs this link—a “narrow banking” experiment—reduces profits and aggregate welfare substantially, underscoring the value of integrated intermediation.

The Funding Channel of Monetary Policy

with Germán Gutiérrez and Hossein Poorvasei (draft coming soon)

We study the funding channel of monetary policy, in which policy affects bank credit by reshaping the prices and quantities of all bank liabilities. Using new evidence on the curvature of wholesale funding supply and banks’ ability to substitute across instruments, we show that monetary pass-through is weak in normal times—when wholesale supply is flat and substitution is easy—but strong in stress, when wholesale supply becomes steep and the marginal dollar of funding is wholesale. A simple sufficient statistic links lending pass-through to (i) the share of marginal funding sourced from wholesale markets and (ii) the slope of their inverse supply, net of deposit substitution. Counterfactuals varying the liquidity regime imply sizable differences in the effectiveness of rate policy and of liquidity tools.
